Mircea Eliade

Here is a timeline of Mircea Eliade's life:

  • 1907: Mircea Eliade is born on the 9th of March in Bucharest, Romania, as the second son to captain Gheorghe Eliade and to Ioana Eliade. Initially, his father's name was Ieremia, and he originated from Tecuci.
  • 1914: He moves to Bucharest, where he attends the school in Mântuleasa street.
  • 1917: He is admitted to Spiru Haret high school.
  • 1921: 12 May, Mircea Eliade makes his debut with The Enemy of the Silk Worm. It was signed Eliade Gh. Mircea.
  • 1923: He learns Italian so as to read Papini in the original and English so as to read Frazer. He starts learning Hebrew and Persian.
  • 1925: Eliade is a student in philosophy at the Faculty of Letters and Philosophy from Bucharest.
  • 1927: His first trip to Italy. He visits Papini, who had a great influence on the young Eliade.
  • 1928: In October he graduates the University of Bucharest with a paper on Campanella. 20 November he leaves for India. 25 November - 5 December he makes a trip to Egypt. On 26 December he arrives to Calcutta.
  • 1930: From January untill September he lives in Calcutta where he meets Maitreyi.
  • 1931: December, he leaves India and returns to Romania.
  • 1933: Mircea Eliade receives his PhD in philosophy with The Compared History of the Yoga Techniques.
  • 1936: Between July and August he travels to London, Oxford, Berlin.
  • 1940: He leaves for London as a cultural attaché.
  • 1941: From 10 February untill 1944 he is cultural adviser in Lisabon.
  • 1945: 16 September, he moves to Paris with Giza, his daughter.
  • 1948: He starts his collaboration with the magazine Critique, under the command of Georges Bataille.
  • 1949: 15 July Mircea Eliade makes a trip to Italy, where he writes 300 pages of his novel Forêt Interdite.
  • 1950: He takes part in The International Congress of the History of Religions from Amsterdam.
  • 1952: In May he travels to Italy again.
  • 1956: On 1 October he goes to Chicago.
  • 1957: He moves to Chicago as a professor of history of religions at the University of Chicago.
  • 1960: In September Mircea Eliade takes part in The Congress of the History of Religions from Marburg.
  • 1964: He is given the title Sewele L. Avery Distinguished Service Professor.
  • 1966: 11 May he becomes a member of The American Academy of Arts and Sciences.
  • 1970: August - September he travels to Sweden and Norway and takes part in the Congress of the History of Religions.
  • 1977: Mircea Eliade is given the Bordin award by the French Academy.
  • 1985: He becomes Doctor Honoris Causa of the University of Washington.
  • 1986: 22 April, at 9 am, Mircea Eliade dies and he is incinerated the next day in Rockefeller Chapel from Hyde Park. His grave is located in Oak Woods Cemetery.

"Like Freud and Jung and Rudolf Otto, all of whom contributed deep strands to his work, Eliade argued boldly for universals where he might more safely have argued for widely prevalent patterns. Yet many of the patterns that he identified in religions that spanned the entire globe and the whole of human history — a span that no one has ever known as well as he did — inspired an entire generation of both scholars and amateurs of the study of religion, and they still prove useful as starting points for the comparative study of religion and still hold water even after the challenges posed by new data to which Eliade did not have access. His concept of hierophany, the sudden irruption of the sacred in the profane world, sacred time opening to the transcendent, resulting in radical discontinuities, has proved a far more widely applicable and heuristic term than the older, narrower term "theophany," denoting the manifestation of a god. And his argument that religious forms, particularly myths, are usefully studied in popular culture as well as in the great scriptures is a postmodern idea that he formulated long before postmodernism. He taught us that myths (and, to a great extent, rituals) retold and reenacted in the present transport the worshipper back to the world of origins, the world of events that took place in illo tempore, "in that time"; this basic idea of what he called (after Nietzsche) "the eternal return" has become a truism in the study of religion and does, I think, apply to many mythologies, though not, as Eliade claimed, to all. His ideas about the alternation and interaction of cosmos and chaos, and cyclical/mythical time and linear/historical time, the sacred and the profane, are similarly fruitful starting points for many, if not all, cultures. Above all, his insistence that it is possible to find meaningful synchronic patterns of symbolism in addition to the phenomena that are unique to each time and place — this is the foundation on which the entire field of comparative religion still stands, and Eliade laid the cornerstone."

--- Wendy Doniger in her "Foreword" to the 2004 edition of Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy by Mircea Eliade, as translated from French by Willard R. Trask.
